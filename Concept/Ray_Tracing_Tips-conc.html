Ray Tracing Tips

ASAP begins tracing rays from the point at which they were defined. Inappropriate starting points can give rise to unexpected results. This topic provides tips on ray tracing.


  • Make sure the rays are traced from a location involving a nonzero propagation distance to the next object.
  • ASAP intersects and propagates rays according to the geometry of the system, the SEARCH command settings, and the interfaces of the objects.
  • ASAP seeks out the physically correct propagation path, even if it is not necessarily the one you want.
  • Rays must initially intersect the first or last conicoid of an object composed of lenses and then they must intersect the intervening conicoids sequentially. If they do not, you may generate unreal ray trajectories.
  • Objects composed of lenses are not sealed volumes and so it is possible to propagate rays through an object as if it were not there.
  • The ray trace algorithm in ASAP is essentially a smart polynomial root solver designed to quickly locate the shortest intersection distance to the next valid object. All propagation distances must involve a positive distance.
  • A common ray-tracing mistake is to define a ray grid or move a set of rays such that they are nominally coincident with an object. This action has the following effects on the ray trace algorithm:
    1. Random chance determines whether or not the rays intersect the object. In all likelihood, some of the rays do not intersect the object and, therefore, follow spurious trajectories.
    2. Since ASAP determines refractive indices at an interface on the fly, rays that do not intersect the object may not have refractive indices that make sense at the next object intersection.
    3. Although it is unlikely, ASAP could get stuck in a loop in the intersection routine trying to decide if, in fact, it has intersected the object.
