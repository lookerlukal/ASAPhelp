Gaussian Beam Superposition Algorithm

The Gaussian beam superposition algorithm performs a variety of different types of analyses on general optical systems. NOTE: These techniques should not be used when the wavelength of your source is on the order of the size of your object structures.

Most of the limitations of the algorithm to correctly predict the optical field can be traced to parabasal ray violations of the paraxial wave equation. This problem occurs when the parabasal rays diverge significantly from the trajectory of the base ray and therefore no longer model the local field. The solution to this problem may be to DECOMPOSE the field into a new rayset.

ASAP tolerates a certain amount of departure of the parabasal rays from the base ray before a warning occurs. However, it is entirely possible that, even after several warnings, the resultant calculation is still correct.

Warning Messages

The three warning messages that ASAP can print while performing the SPREAD or FIELD calculations are listed below. These are warnings, not errors - the analyses may still be valid but should be viewed with some caution.


  • DEPARTURE FROM PARAXIAL APPROXIMATION FOR BEAM xxx IS yyy WAVES

    The complex paraxial optical invariant should be equal to zero if the parabasal rays remain in a linear region about the base ray. As it sometimes happens for a variety of reasons, the parabasal rays begin to diverge and are no longer linear. This message is printed whenever the complex paraxial invariant exceeds 0.1 waves. This message is also documented on the Knowledge Base.

  • MAXIMUM DEPARTURE FROM ORTHOGONAL RAY SET FOR BEAM xxx IS yyy WAVES

    In addition to the complex paraxial optical invariant, there are several orthogonal invariants that should also be equal to zero if the parabasal rays remain in a linear region about the base ray. This message is printed whenever any of these invariants exceeds 0.1 waves.

  • POSITIVITY VIOLATION AT SAMPLE ORIGIN FOR BEAM xxx:

    The irradiance of the Gaussian beam should fall off as a function of radial distance from the center of the base ray. There are instances when the Gaussian irradiance increases with radial distance, which is not a physical situation.
