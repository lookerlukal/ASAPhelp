Polarization Ray Tracing Overview

FRESNEL BOTH initiates polarization calculations by instructing ASAP to calculate the appropriate reflection and transmission coefficients at an OBJECT INTERFACE for each ray as a function of polarization and angle of incidence.

Performing a Polarization Ray Trace

To perform a polarization ray trace, you must configure the system geometry and sources for the ray trace. The primary method of configuring the system geometry for a polarization ray trace is to issue a FRESNEL BOTH command.

FRESNEL has many options including the ability to calculate the reflection and transmission coefficients independently as a function of either an s or p state or an incoherent average of the two. FRESNEL BOTH is required for a complete polarization ray trace. The FRESNEL command can be issued at your discretion and only affects the ray trace.

Configuring the Source for Polarization Ray Trace

To configure the source, you must add a physical property to the source with the POLARIZ command, which sets up the initial polarization state of the rays. The command parameters are very similar to the Jones Calculus format.

In the ASAP unpolarized mode of operation, the optical field U is a scalar quantity that is solely a function of position. The field is therefore written as:

U = U(q,r)

where q, r are arbitrary coordinates. When polarization analysis is requested, the optical field becomes a complex vector quantity of the form:

U = (aq + a'r) U(q,r)

where q, r are orthogonal unit vectors and a,a' are complex amplitudes in the two orthogonal directions. It follows that the direction of propagation k is given by:

k = q x r

The variables, a and a' may be interpreted as Jones vector coefficients if the model is set up in a specific orientation.

Recognizing that the ASAP polarization formalism is similar in structure to that of Jones calculus, it is not surprising that ASAP shares its limitations. Therefore, when polarization ray tracing is requested, ASAP can only propagate coherent beams that are completely polarized. Unpolarized beams must be synthesized by superimposing beams of (user-specified) random polarization states.

Note: Polarized light is widely discussed in the general optics literature. Note the topic of sign conventions. An excellent discussion is contained in section 8.12 of Optics by Eugene Hecht or the out-of-print book, Polarized Light: Production and Use by William A. Shurcliff.
Background of Ray Tracing Analysis

Ray tracing analysis techniques traditionally have assumed unpolarized light and ignored the polarization sensitivity of optical interfaces and coatings. In more recent years however, the demands for accurate assessment of the polarization state of light passing through an optical system have grown in support of the design of increasingly sensitive instrumentation. Consequently, most comprehensive optical design/analysis software have this analysis capability.

Two optical techniques have been used over the years for the characterization of polarized light: Jones Calculus and Mueller Calculus. ASAP uses neither of them, although the Jones calculus is close to what ASAP does:


  • Jones Calculus

    The Jones calculus was designed specifically for purely polarized light and is mathematically compact: polarizing elements are described by a complex 2x2 matrix (the Jones matrix) and the polarization state vector is a 2-vector (the Jones vector). While much simpler to manipulate, the penalty is of course the amount of complex arithmetic.

  • Mueller Calculus

    The Mueller calculus is more general than Jones calculus, since it can describe both polarized and unpolarized light and everything in between. The penalty for this generality is the verbosity of the mathematics: polarizing elements are described by a 4x4 matrix (the Mueller matrix) and the polarization state vector is a 4-vector (the Stokes vector). Tracing the polarization state through a system involves a series of 4x4 matrix multiplications at each surface intersection for each ray that is traced.
