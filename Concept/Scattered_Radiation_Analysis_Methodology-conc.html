Scattered Radiation Analysis Methodology

The goal in a classical stray light analysis usually is to determine the amount of unwanted scattered radiation propagating to a detector in order to assess its effect on system performance.

The unwanted scattered radiation may come from signal beams that you are trying to detect or from noise sources. It may even be thermally generated. The optical elements may scatter radiation to the detector from the signal or noise sources. The noise sources may interact with optical components and mechanical structures causing scattered radiation. In any situation a basic three-step process is followed to determine the amount of scattered radiation getting to a detector.


  • The objects seen by the detector are identified. These are called the critical objects. They are critical objects because only radiation scattering from them propagates to the detector.
  • The objects directly illuminated by a primary source of unwanted radiation are identified. These are called the illuminated objects.
  • After the critical and illuminated objects are identified, then any paths connecting the illuminated objects, critical objects, and the detector are determined. ASAP may then be used to calculate the amount of unwanted radiation scattered to the detector using in-field and out-of-field scattering analysis techniques.
