Harvey Model

Most materials and surface treatments that are used in illumination applications fit the modified Harvey model, or a sum or two Harvey models.

Examples include vacuum-metalized surfaces, plastic injection-molded parts, surfaces roughened by means of sand blasting or acid treatment, white paint, and white paper.

Surfaces that require more advanced modeling include black paint and surfaces with a distinct roughness pattern, such as parallel grooves on an extruded part.

In the analysis of many illumination systems, a Monte Carlo ray trace is desirable (one ray in, one ray out). In this type of analysis, a ray incident on any surface reflects or transmits in a random direction. The flux of that ray is normalized, based on the scatter model associated with that surface. When this occurs - and if the optical surface is more specular than diffuse - the surface roughness can be defined using the ASAP command, ROUGHNESS MODEL, which is easy to implement.

If you need to describe surface scatter as a function, the Harvey model is quite useful. The Harvey model is easy to define and fits well to most surfaces. If the surface is near specular, you can use the ROUGHNESS MODEL command in ASAP. The result is a simple, straightforward approach for applying scatter properties in an analysis.
