Anisotropic Polarizing Elements Overview

Retardance (sometimes called birefringence) is the property of an interface, where the Optical Path Length (OPL) or phase change between the orthonormal components, which mathematically describe the polarization state, is a function of the incident polarization state. Many polarizing optical elements, including waveplates and some polarizers, work on this basis.

Stress-induced birefringence is another example of retardance. In these cases the electric field components experience a different index of refraction depending upon the propagation direction of the optical field within the material. Such materials are called anisotropic.

ASAP has the ability to simulate anisotropic materials that are uniaxial; that is, the materials have two different indices of refraction that depend on the propagation direction of the optical field within the material. The indices are typically referred to as ordinary and extraordinary. The ordinary and extraordinary indices are defined using the data base modifier, MEDIA CRYSTAL.

The IDEAL lens command is for modeling polarizing elements in a macroscopic sense with the Jones calculus format.

In many situations, the birefringence introduces a spatially dependent, electric field apodization that is not easily plotted with the PLOT POLARIZATION command. This difficulty occurs because ASAP actually splits a ray at these interfaces, thereby creating an extraordinary ray in addition to the ordinary ray.

SPLIT may be used to control the number of extraordinary rays created.

Although FIELD automatically accounts for the vector nature of the optical field in such things as PSF calculations, you can use the FIELD. . .DELTA option to plot the electric field vectors as a function of time in these situations.
