Stray Light - Calculating Radiation Scattered to Detector

ASAP may be used directly to calculate the amount of radiation scattered to the detector when the critical and illuminated objects are known, as well as the paths connecting them to the detector.

In out-of-field situations, it is crucial that important areas are properly configured within your system model. For example, important areas are often pupil sizes and placed at pupil locations to force scattered radiation in the general direction of the detector. At this point in the stray light analysis, the critical and illuminated objects should have the appropriate scattering models assigned to them.

Initially, trace only a small number of rays from the scatter source to verify that the system geometry and associated scatter commands were entered correctly. When the preliminary calculations are complete, you may trace a large number (and scatter a large) number of rays to obtain an accurate calculation of the scattered radiation propagating to the detector plane. The PATHS command is especially useful for assessing scattered ray paths and their associated scattered power.

ASAP may be used to perform a first-order stray light analysis of power scattered to the detector. If this quantity is multiplied by the ratio of the area of the collector to the source, you obtain the GCF from the source to the collector as a function of power of, or on, the source. Therefore by tracing rays from the detector, using the STATS POSITION command, and the appropriate source and collector areas, the GCF from the source to the collector is determined. If you now trace rays into the system with an appropriate normalization, the STATS POSITION command may be used to calculate the power on each source object. Again apply the BSDF form of the power transfer equation to calculate the power scattered to the detector.

If thermal emission is important in your analysis, then multiply each GCF from collector to the source by the Stephan-Boltzman constant, the temperature to the fourth power, the object emissivity, and the collector area to obtain the thermally emitted power scattered to the detector.



Note:

This is a first-order analysis of the power scattered to the detector, which should get to within a factor of three to four of an actual ASAP analysis. If it does not, re-evaluate your analysis.

In general, predicting the actual power scattered to the detector to within a factor of two to three is good.
